Math scores plummeted across New York City, but the drop was not equally felt

By Alice Tecotzky

September 28th, 2022

Photo by Kate Della Pietra

New York City has not understood the full scope of how pandemic-era learning has affected students’ reading and math performances—until now. On Wednesday, the Department of Education finally released the first comprehensive standardized test scores for students in grades three through eight since 2019, after refusing to publish the data for two years.

The DOE released a broad overview of score data from the 2021-22 school year, publishing citywide scores broken down by demographic and age group. Across the city, math scores dropped by 7.7 percent since 2019, while reading scores increased by 1.6 percent. Overall, almost half of the city’s students passed their reading exams, while fewer than 38 percent passed their math tests. Yet gaps in testing endure: longstanding score discrepancies persist between white and Asian students and their Black and Hispanic peers.

In total, only 20.6 percent of Black students and 23.3 percent of Hispanic students passed their math tests, while 58.5 percent of white students and 68.3 percent of Asian students passed. Math scores dipped by 9.9, 8.2, 7.7, and 6.1 percent among Hispanic, white, Black, and Asian students respectively.

Reading proficiency rose among each racial demographic included in the data, inching up by 0.3, 0.7, 0.8, and 2.6 percent for Hispanic, white, Black, and Asian students respectively.

While the city did not release district-by-district data, the demographic gaps in testing may indicate disparities between districts, although other factors, such as poverty rates, can also impact scores. The standardized test scores from 2019 demonstrate that students in Manhattan’s District 5—which encompasses much of Upper Manhattan, beginning at West 122nd Street and stretching up to just beyond West 155th Street—fared worse than those citywide.

In 2019, 31 percent of students in District 5 passed their reading tests, compared to 47.4 percent citywide. That year, 26 percent of students in District 5 passed their math tests, while 45.6 percent of all students passed.

As of the 2021-22 academic year, 46.5 percent of students were Black, 39.4 percent were Hispanic, 6.8 percent were white, and 3.7 percent were Asian in District 5. The DOE also characterized 82.1 percent of students as “facing economic hardship,” a category that takes into account a child’s housing stability and family income, among other factors. In Manhattan, however, 22.7 percent of students were Black, 44.8 percent were Hispanic, 16.1 percent were white, and 11.9 percent were Asian.

“I think part of the reason for such a big divide is lack of education,” Shaneeka Wilson, co-chair of the youth, education, and libraries subcommittee of Community Board 9, said. “[That] has a social and obviously economical effect on families.”

Wilson mentioned that in predominantly Black and Hispanic communities like District 5, many parents care for multiple children as well as their own parents, thereby stretching incomes and limiting the money they can spend on tutoring and other supplemental educational programs. Ultimately, though, Wilson attributes the persistent educational disparities to the Department of Education itself.

“I feel very disappointed in the DOE, and the mayor, and even in our state government,” Wilson said. “It just sucks. It’s not really a great place to be in terms of trying to close some of those racial divides.”
